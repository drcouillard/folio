World Geography · Grade 9 · Unit 1
Here's a strange thought: every map you've ever seen is a lie.
Not a malicious one. An unavoidable one. The Earth is a sphere. Paper is flat. No matter how clever your math, you cannot perfectly flatten a sphere without tearing, stretching, or squishing something. Every map trades one kind of accuracy for another.

"A map is not the territory — it's a simplified model designed to answer a specific question. The question determines which lies are acceptable."

That's what this unit is about. Not memorizing where things are — but learning to read the language maps use to represent a world too big and curved to fit on a page. By the end, you'll know how to ask the right questions before you trust any map.

Part 2 of 3 · Worked example
Reading a map like a navigator
In 1569, Gerardus Mercator published a map that changed ocean travel forever. A sailor could draw a straight line between two ports and follow that single compass bearing the whole way. Here's how to use map scale step by step.
Finding real distance from a map
1
Find the map's scale. Our map shows 1 cm = 100 km (ratio 1:10,000,000).
2
Measure the distance. London to Cairo measures 35 mm on this map.
3
Convert units first: 35 mm = 3.5 cm. Multiply: 3.5 × 100 km = 350 km... but wait.
4
Check your projection. On Mercator, a straight line is a constant compass bearing — not the shortest path on Earth. The actual shortest route is 3,500 km. The map didn't lie — you just needed to know what question it was built to answer.
Before you use any map, ask: what was this built to show? That tells you what to trust — and what to verify.
